Sick leave clamp fails to cure HSE's absentee crisis . ABSENTEEISM amongst the country's health workers has increased since last year, despite a clampdown on chronic sick leave crippling the health service. Monthly absenteeism bulletins are now being collated in a bid to curb the practice which costs the agency 150m a year in replacement staff. While sick leave rates are always expected to increase during the winter months, absenteeism rates will not be helped by the current pressures on health service staff, who claim they are overworked and under resourced. Despite the pressures on front line nursing staff, they take less sick leave than their colleagues in portering, catering, community welfare and other anicillary health services. Sick leave rates for nurses average at six per cent across the health service, compared to more than seven per cent for those in anicillary and general support services. The HSE has said that staff, including support staff, had to do a lot of manual work, which leaves them more prone to injury. Limerick hospitals recorded particularly high rates of absenteeism in several departments.
