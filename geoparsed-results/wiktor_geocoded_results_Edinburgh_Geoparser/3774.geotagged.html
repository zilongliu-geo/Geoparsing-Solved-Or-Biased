Binda is a village in the Southern Tablelands region of New South Wales, Australia in Upper Lachlan Shire. At the 2011 census, Binda had a population of 211 people. It is about 17 km north-north-west of Crookwell in the county of Georgiana. Other near-by towns or locations are: Crooked Corner Peelwood Redground Wheeo Narrawa North The origin of the name is thought to be from the Gandangara Aboriginal word for deep water. The site of Binda was explored in 1820 by James Meehan and John Oxley and the first pastoral runs were taken by Francis Oakes, Chief Constable of Parramatta, Rowland Hassall and Thomas Bray in 1825 and 1826. The town was a listed locality in the census of 1828. The town was gazetted in 1850 with subdivision for sale in 1852 making it the oldest town in Crookwell Shire and the rich Pastoral leases and discovery of gold at Tuena, 33 kilometres to the north caused the town to grow quickly, with a School (1851), post office (1852), Court of Petty Sessions (1863), and Anglican church(1872) following soon. The town reach about a thousand persons in the 1870s.
